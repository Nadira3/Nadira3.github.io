<h4>1. Presentation & Initial Assessment</h4>
<details>
<summary><strong>A. History Taking</strong></summary>

- Onset & Duration  
  - Acute: < 2 wks  
  - Subacute: 2‚Äì6 wks  
  - Chronic: > 6 wks (‚â• 4 wks in adults ‚Üí malignancy workup)

- Associated Symptoms

  - Systemic: fever, weight loss, night sweats (lymphoma, TB)

- Thyroid Function Symptoms  
  - Palpitations, tremor, heat intolerance ‚Äî absent here

- Risk Factors & Exposures  
  - Prior neck irradiation, family history of thyroid cancer

- Social Hx  
  - Tobacco, alcohol (‚Üë head & neck malignancy risk)
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>B. Vital Signs & Labs</strong></summary>

  - Standing: 102 bpm (no orthostasis)  
  - Persistent tachycardia in euthyroid ‚Üí consider anxiety, anemia, POTS

- Thyroid Function Tests  
  - TSH & Free T‚ÇÑ: normal ‚Üí euthyroid

- CBC  
  - Rule out anemia (tachycardia cause)
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>C. Physical Examination</strong></summary>

- Inspection  
  - Midline swelling, smooth, ~3 cm  
  - Moves with swallowing ‚Üí thyroid origin

- Palpation  
  - Firm, non-tender  
  - Mobile except on deglutition  
  - No pulsatility or bruit (rules out vascular)

- Special Tests  
  - Transillumination: negative (solid vs cystic)
</details>

<h4>2. Anatomy & Clinical Reasoning</h4>
<details>
<summary><strong>Why Thyroid Mass Moves on Swallowing</strong></summary>

- Thyroid enclosed in pretracheal fascia ‚Üí attaches to larynx/trachea  
- Elevation of larynx/trachea during deglutition transmits movement to gland  
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Why Thyroglossal Cyst Moves with Tongue Protrusion</strong></summary>

- Remnant tract from foramen cecum to thyroid  
- Attached to hyoid/tongue base ‚Üí protrusion pulls cyst upward  
</details>

### 11. Goitre Classification  

#### 11.1 Simple (Non-Toxic) Goitre  
<details>
<summary><strong>Definition & Subtypes</strong></summary>

- Diffuse Goitre  
  - Uniform, smooth enlargement  
  - Euthyroid  
  - ‚Üë iodine deficiency (endemic)

- Solitary Nodular Goitre  
  - Single palpable nodule  
  - Colloid cyst, adenoma, or carcinoma

- Multinodular Goitre (MNG)  
  - Multiple discrete nodules  
  - Asymmetric, heterogeneous  
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Clinical Correlations</strong></summary>

1. Diffuse Goitre  
   > 22-year-old from iodine-deficient region; 2-year painless bilateral swelling; euthyroid; treated with iodized salt ‚Üí regression in 6 months.

2. Solitary Nodule  
   > 45-year-old man; 1.5 cm right thyroid nodule; US: hypoechoic; FNAC Bethesda II; periodic US follow-up.

3. MNG  
   > 60-year-old woman; 20-year goitre, mild dysphagia; dominant 3 cm nodule FNAC benign; subtotal thyroidectomy for compression.
</details>

### 12 Differentiating Primary vs. Secondary Thyrotoxicosis  

| Feature                     | Primary Thyrotoxicosis      | Secondary Thyrotoxicosis     |
|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|------------------------------|
| TSH Level               | Suppressed (<0.01)          | Normal‚Äìelevated              |
| Eye Signs               | Common (exophthalmos)       | Rare                         |
| Goitre Appearance       | Diffuse or nodular          | Mild diffuse                |
| Autoimmune Markers      | Anti-TSHR, anti-TPO positive| Absent                       |
| RAI Uptake              | Diffuse/focal (‚Äúhot‚Äù nodules)| Diffuse with non-suppressed TSH |
| Pituitary MRI           | Normal                      | Adenoma visible              |

<details>
<summary><strong>12.1 Pathophysiology</strong></summary>

- Primary: Gland overproduces hormone (Graves‚Äô or toxic nodules)  
- Secondary: Pituitary TSH-oma drives normal gland  
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>12.2 Clinical & Biochemical</strong></summary>

- Primary: TSH‚Üì, T‚ÇÑ/T‚ÇÉ‚Üë; eye signs; autoimmune markers  
- Secondary: TSH ‚Üó or normal, T‚ÇÑ/T‚ÇÉ‚Üë; pituitary mass  
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>12.3 Imaging & Tests</strong></summary>

- TRH Test: Blunted TSH rise in primary; paradoxical in secondary  
- RAI Scan: Homogeneous/focal uptake primary; diffuse non-suppressed in secondary  
- MRI: Pituitary adenoma in secondary  
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>12.4 Management</strong></summary>

- Primary: Methimazole/PTU ‚Üí RAI or thyroidectomy for definitive

### 20. Colostomy  

#### 20.1 Types of Colostomy  
<details>
<summary><strong>End Colostomy</strong></summary>

- Definition: Proximal end of colon brought out as single stoma; distal segment oversewn or resected  
- Indication: Permanent diversion for unresectable distal disease (e.g., obstructive anorectal carcinoma)  
- Case:  
  > 72-year-old man with inoperable rectal cancer and complete obstruction ‚Üí end colostomy for palliation  
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Loop Colostomy</strong></summary>

- Definition: Loop of colon (transverse or sigmoid) opened and matured as two lumens (proximal stool, distal mucus)  
- Indications:  
  - Temporary protection of distal anastomosis  
  - Palliative diversion with planned future reversal  
- Case:  
  > 58-year-old woman post‚Äìlow anterior resection receives protective loop sigmoid colostomy to allow anastomotic healing  
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Double-Barrel Colostomy</strong></summary>

- Definition: Colon completely divided; both ends matured as separate stomas (proximal stool, distal mucus)  
- Indication: Decompression of distal bowel with future reconnection planned  
- Case:  
  > 45-year-old man with perforated diverticulitis and peritonitis ‚Üí transverse double-barrel colostomy for source control  
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Site Selection: Transverse vs. Sigmoid</strong></summary>

- Transverse Colon: More mobile, emergency accessibility ‚Üí loop/double-barrel in acute obstruction  
- Sigmoid Colon: Preferred for elective stomas ‚Üí patient comfort, firmer stool output  
</details>
