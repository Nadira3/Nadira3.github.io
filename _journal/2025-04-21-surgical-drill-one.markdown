    <h1 class="case-title">Clinical Case Presentation</h1>
    <div class="case-subtitle">Abdominal Pain Case Study</div>
  </div>
  
  <div class="case-description">
    <p>A 45-year-old male presents with <strong>severe upper abdominal pain</strong> for the last 12 hours. The pain is constant, radiating to his back, and worsens with movement. He denies any nausea or vomiting. He has a history of chronic alcohol use, but denies any recent episodes of binge drinking. His vital signs are stable, but his blood pressure is slightly elevated at 140/90 mmHg.</p>
    
    <p>He has a noticeable tenderness in the upper abdomen, particularly in the epigastric region. The rest of his examination is unremarkable.</p>
    
    <div class="red-herring">
      <p>The patient mentions having a mild headache for the past two days, but denies any fever, neck stiffness, or visual disturbances.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

What's the rationale behind each question? And why is the headache potentially irrelevant here? Show me you're thinking critically, or you'll fall flat.</p>

      <div class="collapsible-content student-content">
        <p>Presenting complaint is abdominal pain. Given history suggests site of pain is in the epigastric region. I'll ask how the pain started, if it was gradual or sudden, I'll ask if he has had a previous experience or if this is a first time occurrence.</p>
        <p>I'll ask him to describe the pain. Burning pain may suggest PUD. I'll ask for associated symptoms I'll expect to see in PUD or perforated ulcer. That's all I know.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="collapsible">
      <div class="collapsible-header consultant-header">
        <div class="collapsible-title">

        <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg">
          <path d="M6 9L12 15L18 9" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"/>
        </svg>
      </div>
      <div class="collapsible-content consultant-content">
        <p>You're already making mistakes, and we haven't even started the real questioning yet. How did you arrive at that? Just because you know some basic associations doesn't mean you're thinking deeply enough. You think you can just start asking vague questions like "How did it start?" and be done with it? You're not playing detective, you're playing with people's lives.</p>
        
        <div class="message-point">
          <span class="highlight">Pain onset:</span> Fine, you're thinking about whether it was sudden or gradual, but why? You need to be specific. You're looking for clues about the nature of the pain. Sudden onset could suggest an acute process, like an infarction or perforation. Gradual onset could suggest something like gastritis or an obstructive issue. You need to rationalize why that question is going to help you rule things in or out.
        </div>
        
        <div class="message-point">
          <span class="highlight">Previous episodes:</span> You mentioned asking about prior episodes, good. Why is that important? Chronic pancreatitis, for example, might have had similar episodes before. But you didn't think about that in your differential, did you?
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>
    
    <div class="collapsible">
      <div class="collapsible-header student-header">
        <div class="collapsible-title">
          <div class="message-avatar student-avatar">S</div>
          <span>Student's Response to Criticism</span>
        </div>
        <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg">
          <path d="M6 9L12 15L18 9" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"/>
        </svg>
      </div>
      <div class="collapsible-content student-content">
        <p>You're right. I need to be more systematic and thoughtful in my approach.</p>
        
        <p>For pain onset, knowing if it was sudden versus gradual helps differentiate between acute processes like perforation or pancreatic inflammation versus more chronic conditions. Sudden severe pain suggests pancreatic inflammation, perforated ulcer, or possibly even mesenteric ischemia.</p>
        
        <p>Regarding previous episodes, this helps establish if we're dealing with an acute or chronic condition. Recurrent episodes might suggest chronic pancreatitis especially with the alcohol history provided.</p>
      </div>
